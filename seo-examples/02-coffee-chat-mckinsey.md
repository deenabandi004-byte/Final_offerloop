# McKinsey Meeting Prep: Questions, Research & a Done-For-You Prep Doc

**Meta title:** McKinsey Meeting Prep, Questions + Free Prep Doc

A strong McKinsey meeting comes down to three things: a few sharp questions about the consultant's actual path, ten minutes of research on their practice, and a follow-up that gets remembered. Below is a preview of the prep doc Offerloop builds for you, for the specific consultant you're meeting, not a generic one.

## The problem: generic prep doesn't survive contact

You can find a list of "meeting questions" anywhere. What you can't easily do is prep for *this* consultant, their practice, their office, the McKinsey insight they'd expect you to have read, the recent work their team published. Generic prep produces a generic conversation, and consultants are trained to notice exactly that.

## A preview of your McKinsey meeting prep doc

This is what Offerloop generates, here's a sample for a hypothetical McKinsey consultant in the LA office:

> **Questions to ask (pick 4–6):**
> 1. What actually got you the McKinsey offer over other final-round candidates?
> 2. What does a normal Monday look like on a typical study?
> 3. What's McKinsey's current point of view on AI in your clients' industries?
> 4. Given my interest in operations work, who else at McKinsey should I talk to?
>
> **Research before the chat:**
> - Their LinkedIn path + any panel or podcast they've done
> - McKinsey Global Institute's two most recent reports in their practice
> - Where McKinsey wins vs. BCG/Bain on the kind of work they do
>
> **Skip these questions:** "What's it like to work at McKinsey?" · "What advice would you give?" · anything about pay · anything Google-able.
>
> **Follow-up:** 4-sentence thank-you within 24 hours referencing one specific thing they said. Then a 6-week update email, almost nobody sends it, and consultants notice.

## How long should the chat be?

15 minutes for a first chat. One minute of intros, eight to ten minutes of your questions, the rest their feedback and next steps. Running over signals you can't manage time, the one thing a consultant is trained to catch.
