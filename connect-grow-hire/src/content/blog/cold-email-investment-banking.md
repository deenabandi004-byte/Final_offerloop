## Why Networking Is Critical for IB Recruiting

Investment banking recruiting is one of the most competitive processes in any industry. At bulge bracket firms like Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley, acceptance rates for summer analyst programs hover around 1 to 3 percent. At elite boutiques like Evercore and Centerview, the numbers are even lower.

Here is what most students do not realize: **the majority of IB interviews are influenced by internal referrals and networking.** Submitting an online application without any internal connections is essentially sending your resume into a black hole. Recruiters at top banks receive thousands of applications and lean heavily on employee referrals to filter candidates.

Cold emailing is the most scalable way to build those connections, especially if you are at a semi-target or non-target school where on-campus recruiting pipelines are limited.

## Who to Email at Bulge Bracket vs. Boutique Firms

Your targeting strategy should differ based on the type of bank:

### Bulge Bracket Banks (Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citi)

- **First and second-year analysts:** Most responsive to cold outreach. They remember recruiting vividly and often enjoy advising students.
- **Alumni in any group:** Shared university is your strongest advantage. Even if they are in a different product group or industry coverage team than you are targeting, they can provide valuable advice and warm introductions.
- **Analysts in your target group:** If you are specifically interested in TMT or healthcare, reach out to analysts on those teams.

### Elite Boutiques (Evercore, Centerview, PJT, Lazard, Moelis)

- **The same targeting rules apply,** but boutiques are smaller, so your network of potential contacts is narrower. Cast a wider net across the firm rather than limiting yourself to one group.
- **Associates are more accessible at boutiques** because teams are smaller and the culture is often less hierarchical than at bulge brackets.

## Subject Line Formulas That Get Opened

Your subject line is the single most important element of your cold email. If the subject line does not compel the recipient to open the email, nothing else matters.

**Formulas that work:**

- "[University] junior interested in [Bank] [Group]"
- "Fellow [University/Major] alum, quick question about [Bank]"
- "[Mutual connection] recommended I reach out"
- "[University] student, 15 min on your [Bank] TMT experience"

**What to avoid:**

- "Networking" (too vague)
- "Interested in investment banking" (about you, not relevant to them)
- "Can you refer me?" (too aggressive for a first email)
- No subject line at all

## Template 1: Alumni Cold Email for IB

> **Subject:** [University] '[Grad Year] interested in [Bank] [Group]
>
> Hi [First Name],
>
> I'm a [year] at [University] studying [major]. I found your profile in the alumni directory and saw that you're an analyst in [Bank]'s [group] group. I'd love to learn about your experience, especially how you decided on [group] and what your day-to-day looks like.
>
> I've been preparing for IB recruiting and am particularly drawn to [group/bank] because [one specific, genuine reason]. Any insights you could share would be really helpful as I navigate the process.
>
> Would you have 15 minutes for a quick call in the next couple of weeks?
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]
> [University] '[Grad Year] | [Major]
> [LinkedIn URL]

## Timing: When to Send for Maximum Response Rates

Investment bankers have some of the most unpredictable schedules in any profession. Timing your emails well can meaningfully increase your response rate.

**Best days:** Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday. Bankers are often catching up on Monday and mentally checked out by Friday afternoon.

**Best times:** 8:00 AM to 9:30 AM in the recipient's time zone. Many analysts check email first thing in the morning before the day gets chaotic. Some students also find success emailing between 9:00 PM and 10:00 PM, when analysts are wrapping up their day and more likely to respond to non-urgent emails.

**Avoid:** Weekend emails can work for some analysts who are in the office, but many prefer to keep weekends for personal time. When in doubt, schedule your email for Tuesday morning.

## The Follow-Up Sequence

Most students send one email and give up. This is a mistake. A well-timed follow-up can double your response rate.

**Follow-up 1 (5 to 7 days later):**

> Hi [First Name], I wanted to follow up on my note from last week. I know you're busy, so I'll keep this brief. I'd genuinely appreciate even 10 minutes of your time to hear about your experience in [group]. Happy to work around your schedule.

**Follow-up 2 (10 to 14 days after the first follow-up):**

> Hi [First Name], apologies for the persistence. I'll leave this as my last note. If you ever have a few minutes to chat about [Bank]'s [group], I'd be grateful. Either way, thanks for your time.

**After two follow-ups, move on.** Three emails is the maximum. More than that crosses the line from persistent to annoying.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do investment bankers actually respond to cold emails from students?

Yes, many do. First and second-year analysts are the most responsive because they recently went through the same recruiting process. Alumni from your university are especially likely to reply. Expect a 10 to 20 percent response rate with well-personalized emails, higher if you share a school or mutual connection.

### Should I cold email analysts or managing directors at investment banks?

Start with analysts and associates who are 1 to 3 years into their roles. They have the most relevant and recent advice for students, and they are far more accessible than senior bankers. Once you build relationships with junior bankers, they can sometimes introduce you to more senior people internally.

### What should I say in a cold email to a Goldman Sachs analyst?

Keep it under 150 words. Open with who you are and one specific reason you are emailing them (shared school, shared background, or something specific about their group). Ask for a 15-minute phone call. Do not ask for a referral in the first email. Reference their specific group or deal experience if possible.

### How early should I start networking for investment banking internships?

Start networking 6 to 9 months before applications open. For summer analyst internships, this means beginning outreach in the spring of your sophomore year. For full-time roles, start in the fall of your junior year. Early networking gives you time to build genuine relationships before you need to ask for referrals.

### Is it better to cold email or use LinkedIn to reach investment bankers?

Email is significantly more effective than LinkedIn messages for IB networking. LinkedIn messages get lost in a cluttered inbox, and many bankers have connection request notifications turned off. A well-written email to their work address feels more professional and gets a higher response rate.
